Complex organisations need to organise themselves in some way. Reasonably enough, rather than doing so from scratch, they adopt standardised frameworks. Millions, and sometimes hundreds of millions of dollars, can depend on whether an organisation adopts Scrum, PRINCE2, OKRs, or one of the many alternatives.

Consequently, the defensiveness around frameworks deserves study: not particular frameworks, but defensiveness around frameworks in general. And not the fact that people get defensive, which is unsurprising, but *the way the defensiveness plays out*.

This defensiveness is especially interesting in light of a common, but not commonly noticed, fact: creators or early advocates of frameworks are often the very people who later disavow them—not necessarily the original principles, but how the framework was rolled out in practice.

Several figures associated with the Agile movement have criticised what the movement became.

Dave Thomas declared that “Agile is dead,” calling it co-opted and meaningless. Ron Jeffries wrote: “I’ve started calling it ‘Dark Agile’... It’s when the name is used against the basic principles of what we were trying to do.”

Jeff Sutherland and Ken Schwaber of Scrum have both expressed regret over how concepts such as “team commitment” and “velocity” have been weaponised by management to pressure teams, contrary to the framework’s intent. Schwaber has been particularly vocal about commoditised rollouts.

Tim Brown of design thinking has criticised its mindless application in industry: “Design thinking is not a concept, like wallpaper, to be applied.”

The interesting thing is not that they critique their creations. Who has not sometimes regretted what people have done with one’s work? The interesting thing is *the shape* of their criticism. John Chris Jones, a major figure in design methods, is especially revealing:

> “I offered a savage auto-critique of my own work... I reacted against the way methods led to rigidity and insensitivity to human complexity.”

He called this *the machine trap*: design methods becoming a “toolkit of rigid methods” that forced professionals to act like “machines”.

The shared criticism is that their methods became rigid and ossified. To these creators, the framework had opened a way of working that better respected the complexity of reality, especially where previous methods had closed it off. But over time, those same frameworks began to close reality off.

This is most telling in *how* adherents defend them. In a typical conversation, when a client questions the framework—“I think Agile is setting us back here”—the response is often: “You are not doing Agile properly.” That is, the cure for the problems of the framework is *more of the framework*.

In this manner, critique of the framework becomes input to the framework itself. Once this shift occurs, critique becomes almost impossible. Every critique is absorbed as more input.

This is not to say frameworks are bad. It is about how we relate to them.

I have observed that this is often the distinction of an experienced, senior thinker. Their mark is this:

> When criticised about a framework, the instinctive response is to answer as an outsider, not defend from within.

In other words, mature thinkers *use frameworks without needing to live inside them*.

This distinction is not marked by hierarchy, time in service, or working experience. There are highly ranked staff who are trapped within frameworks, just as there are new staff who are free. I have yet to get to the bottom of what makes a person one way than the other.

These observations reach further than frameworks such as Agile and OKRs. It applies to management principles such as RACI, and to the way we divide roles: business analysts, architects, engineers. Some organisations can treat these provisionally. Many cannot.

The risk is that strong ideas or candidates are missed because they do not fit, or that important problems persist because the organisation cannot see that the framework itself has become the problem.

Thus, the problem is not the framework itself, but this:

> The tool we used to see has become the criterion for seeing.
