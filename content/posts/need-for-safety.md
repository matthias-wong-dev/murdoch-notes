Consider a manager faced with this decision: a team member has created software that solves a difficult organisational problem far better than anything available in the market. But relying on it would create a risk: dependence on an individual. Do you adopt it, or do you choose the inferior vendor product for maintainability?

One of the unspoken realities of organisations is that need for safety governs far more than is obvious from the surface. 

I know this personally. When an incident occurs—say, a wrong number is reported in a high-profile context—the first question that appears is "Was I responsible?" It takes all my effort to set that aside and turn instead to the questions I know I need to ask: "What is the impact? What now needs to be done?"

In itself, this is not unusual. What is interesting is that another question keeps coming up during the root cause analysis: "Did I or my team follow the process?" 

Why this question?

We all have a need for safety: emotional, social, physical, psychological, mental. This need shapes behaviour everywhere. But when this need for safety is transposed to the organisation, it undergoes a fundamental shift.

First, it is no longer named as safety. It appears instead as compliance, risk management, governance, roles and responsibilities, and clear process. These are standard management practices, and they can all be legitimate. But the driving heart is this: when something goes wrong, responsibility—and exposure—is diffused.


Second, in the long term, individuals seek safety by *increasing their capacity* to deal with reality. They learn and become more competent. Organisations, by contrast, often seek safety by *reducing reality* into forms that can be managed: simplifying complexity into proxies that could be organised and coordinated.

 In organisations, the mechanisms of safety are externalised into structure. They sit outside any one person. They are distributed across process, governance, reporting lines, and approvals. As a result, the people inside the system may sense the problem while remaining unable to revise the system that generates it.

Third, with individuals, the opportunity to change course is always open: if a behaviour is leading down the wrong direction, an individual who notices it can revise judgement. But in an organisation, the mechanisms of safety are *externalised* into structures. They sit outside any one person, and therefore *un-revisable* by the people within them: they may notice the wrong direction and yet nothing can be done by the people experiencing it. 

The implication is that:

"The need for safety in an organisation mitigates pain by diffusing responsibility. But this means that signals of failures are dissolved by structures before they could turn into decisive correction." 

This is visible in major scandals. Boeing’s 737 MAX crashes killed 346 people. The company faced criminal charges, paid billions in fines and compensation, and entered into agreements with regulators. Yet no senior executive went to jail for the core safety failures. As of late 2025, the criminal case was [dismissed](https://www.npr.org/2025/11/07/nx-s1-5601593/boeing-737-max-crashes) via a non-prosecution agreement.

Or consider the case of the Grenfell Tower fire. The Metropolitan Police [investigated 58 individuals and 19 companies for fraudulent behaviour](https://news.met.police.uk/news/investigation-into-grenfell-tower-tragedy-487374). As of now, none had been prosecuted. 

But this is not the most important point. 

Whilst the Grenfell Tower inquiry clearly pointed out that the root cause was the failure of a fragmented system, the responsibility was so diffused that prosecution is impossible—and there is no designer of the flawed system to prosecute. So whilst the justice system may prosecute criminal behaviour *within* the system, but real cause of the failure could not be prosecuted.

This is the need for safety working at full strength.

We began with the question of whether organisations can have [blind spots]({{< relref “blind-spots.md” >}}). The answer was that all structures, by their nature, create them.  

But not all structures are equal.

What we see here is that the need for safety does not just create blind spots—it pushes organisations toward forms that _reinforce_ them. In particular, structures that dampen painful signals before they can register clearly.

This happens in little ways. Opting for a vendor diffuses the responsibility for risk. But it could have been possible to say "Since this innovation matters enough that we will have to build our capacity to own the risk", but doing so would leave the organisation exposed.

This is not to say that in-house code is always better than vendor software. This is not even to say that safety is not vital. 

Rather, the point is that, in every decision, we have to ask:

*"Are we optimising for safety, or optimising for outcome?*

If we don't decide consciously, the need for safety will decide on our behalf.
