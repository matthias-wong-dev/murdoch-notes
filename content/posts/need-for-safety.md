Consider a manager faced with this decision: a team member has created software that solves a difficult organisational problem far better than anything available in the market. But relying on it would create a risk: dependence on an individual. Do you adopt it, or do you choose the inferior vendor product for maintainability?

One of the unspoken realities of organisations is that the need for safety governs far more than is visible.

I know this personally. When an incident occurs—say, a wrong number is reported in a high-profile context—the first question that appears is: *Was I responsible?* It takes effort to set that aside and turn instead to the questions that matter: *What is the impact? What now needs to be done?*

In itself, this is not unusual. What is interesting is that another question keeps returning: *Did I or my team follow the process?*

Why this question?

We all have a need for safety: emotional, social, physical, psychological. This need shapes behaviour everywhere. But when it is transposed into an organisation, it undergoes a shift in nature.

First, it is no longer named as safety. It appears instead as compliance, risk management, governance, roles and responsibilities, and clear process. These are standard management practices, and they can be legitimate. But at their centre is another function: when something goes wrong, responsibility—and exposure—is diffused.

Second, individuals can seek safety by *increasing their capacity* to deal with reality. Over time, they learn and become more competent. Organisations, by contrast, often seek safety by *reducing reality* into forms that can be managed—simplifying complexity into proxies that can be organised and coordinated.

Most importantly, in an individual, the opportunity to change course remains open. If individuals recognise they are heading in the wrong direction, they can revise judgement. But in an organisation, the mechanisms of safety have been *externalised* into structure. They sit outside any one person, and are therefore *un-revisable* by those within them. Unlike an individual, people in an organisation may experience a problem, yet remain unable to alter the system that produces it because the authority to change structures sits with another person.

The implication is this:

*The need for safety in an organisation mitigates pain by diffusing responsibility, and this diffusion becomes binding through structure.*

This is visible in major scandals. Boeing’s 737 MAX crashes killed 346 people. The company faced criminal charges, paid billions in fines and compensation, and entered into agreements with regulators. Yet no senior executive went to jail for the core safety failures. As of late 2025, the criminal case was [dismissed](https://www.npr.org/2025/11/07/nx-s1-5601593/boeing-737-max-crashes) via a non-prosecution agreement.

Or consider the Grenfell Tower fire. The Metropolitan Police [investigated 58 individuals and 19 companies for fraudulent behaviour](https://news.met.police.uk/news/investigation-into-grenfell-tower-tragedy-487374). As of now, none had been prosecuted. 

But this is not the most important part.

The Grenfell inquiry showed that the root cause was the failure of a fragmented system. Yet that fragmentation is precisely what makes prosecution difficult. Responsibility is so diffused that there is no single “designer of the flawed system” to hold accountable. The justice system can prosecute failures *within* the system, but real cause of the failure could not be prosecuted.

This is the need for safety working at full strength.

We began with the question of whether organisations can have [blind spots]({{< relref "blind-spots.md" >}}). The answer was that all structures, by their nature, create them.

But not all structures are equal.

The need for safety does not merely create blind spots—it pushes organisations toward forms that *reinforce* them. In particular, by diffusing responsibility, structures dampen painful signals before they can register to those authority.

Opting for a vendor diffuses responsibility for risk. Yes, it would be possible to say: *this capability matters enough that we will build the capacity to own its risks ourselves.* But doing so concentrates exposure.

The point is not to neglect safety. Keeping teams safe is vital. And clear processes are fundamental to a functioning organisation. 

Rather, the point is that safety and learning pull in different directions. Learning requires exposure to difficult reality—the very thing safety mechanisms are designed to shield us from.

The task, then, is not to abandon safety, but to recognise this tension and actively negotiate it.

To ask, in each decision:

*"Are we optimising for safety, or optimising for outcome?"*
