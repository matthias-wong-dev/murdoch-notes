Throughout my career, senior clients have often approached me for what I call *glamorous data*—they see an opportunity to utilise linked data to solve a challenging business problem, providing insights into an aspect of the business not otherwise possible.

I always give it a serious attempt. Even if it takes longer than expected, the executive intuition is often genuine and worthwhile to pursue.

Nevertheless, most of the value I have seen on the floor is from what I call *unglamorous data*.

When an organisation has only 10 or 20 people, it is still possible for executives to have an idea of what is happening, and respond in a way that corresponds to real-world feedback. The situation changes when an organisation has 500, or 5000. At that scale, maintaining visibility through hierarchy becomes impractical. Thus scale alone is enough to prevent real-world signals from reaching decision makers.

Data provides a way forward when scale obscures visibility. Leaders cannot govern what they cannot see. In this sense, data is the eyes and ears of modern organisations.

From this perspective, glamorous data is the ability to *see interesting things*, while unglamorous data is the ability to *see anything at all.*

I call this unglamorous because, like ordinary eyesight, it is only noticed when it is failing—no one pays attention to their eyes unless something is wrong. In the same way, when unglamorous data is missing, staff complain about disjointed processes, duplication of effort, siloed information. But when unglamorous data is operative, users are not talking about the data any more: they are back to getting the work done.

At the most fundamental level, unglamorous data appears in small, unremarkable ways in the organisation’s workflows: the finance team receiving notice when invoices are due and teams staying in sync about incidents..

One level higher, the organisation begins to see its business represented in forms it recognises, and can actively interrogate. The interrogation is a slow form of seeing—like when a person looks across different parts of a picture. What is seen may not be anything “insightful” in the ordinary sense, but it is a faithful reflection of what is happening.

Even though the ability to see through data may not *reveal* anything profound, it *is* profound. Once the business can see their processes encoded in stable, faithful form, they begin to ask "Why are we doing this? What happened here? What caused this change? What can we do differently?"

Without this visibility, the answers to these questions are anecdotal and hard to verify.

Therefore:

> The value of data is not that it provides answers; but that it stops people from guessing.

In this vein, an often under-appreciated ability of data is not its ability to tell stories, but its ability to eliminate false ones.

More than once, I have seen leadership groups fall into a false assumption. Reality is complex, and when things are complex, simple narratives are attractive—they describe the symptoms, seem coherent, and are not obviously wrong. But they are mismatched with reality.

In those situations, the work is not to produce new insight, but to reconstruct what is already there—to pinpoint the source of confusion and present a view of the data that dissolves the assumption and reveals a different cause.

There is no glamour in eliminating a false narrative, but it is exactly the capability necessary in an organisation dealing with high-stakes situations: and this is what unglamorous data—data that is silently operative and faithfully representing the business—provides.

Unglamorous data is also where many organisations go astray. Organisations often invest significantly in data capability, yet find the value slow to materialise.

This is often because the data is being asked to be *insightful*, and appears to fall short. But the work is first to make the data *faithful*, and then *operable*.

> Data does not need to be impressive for it to be important.

Leaders, particularly those with a strong vision, will naturally be drawn to glamorous data. It is part of how organisations move forward. In my experience, they are often the same people who recognise the value in data when it is not.

Glamorous data is important. Unglamorous data—ordinary data that is meaningfully operative—is equally so.

Data is the eyes and ears of modern organisations. At scale, it is the means by which [reality remains visible]({{< relref "blind-spots.md" >}}).
