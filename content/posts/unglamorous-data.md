Throughout my career, senior clients have often approached for what I call *glamorous data*—they see an opportunity to utilise linked data to solve a challenging business problem, providing insights into an aspect of the business not otherwise possible. This is what most people mean when they say data is an asset.

I always give them a red-hot go. Even if it takes longer than expected, the executive insight is often genuine and worthwhile to pursue.

Nevertheless, most of the value I have seen on the floor is from what I call *unglamorous data*. 

When an organisation has only 10 or 20 people, it is still possible for executives to have an idea of what is happening, and respond in away that corresponds to real-world feedback. The situation changes when an organisation has 500, or 5000. In the latter, maintaining visibility through hierarchy is impractical, if not impossible. Thus scale, and scale alone, is enough to prevent real-world signals from reaching decision makers.

Data provides a way forward when scale obscures visibility. Leaders cannot govern what they cannot see. In this sense, data is the eyes and ears of modern organisations.

From this perspective, glamorous data is the ability to *see interesting things*, while unglamorous data is the ability to *see anything at all.*

I call this unglamorous because, like ordinary eyesight, it is only noticed when it is failing—no one pays attention to their eyes unless there's something wrong with them. In the same way, when unglamorous data is missing, staff complain about disjointed processes, duplication of effort, siloed-information *et cetera.* But when unglamorous data is *operative*, users are not talking about the data any more: they are too busy getting the work done, which the data is now enabling.

At the most fundamental—but not the least—level, unglamorous data is operative through simple actions in the organisation's workflows: the finance team getting timely notice when the invoices set up by another team are due; the science team staying in sync with the operations team about the latest incidents.

One level higher, the organisation is now able to see its business represented in the data in forms they recognise, and can actively interrogate. The interrogation is a slow form of seeing—like when a person can look at different parts of a picture. What is seen may not be anything "insightful" in the ordinary use of the word, but it is a faithful reflection of what is happening. 

Even though the ability to see through data may not *reveal* anything profound, it *is* profound. Once the business can see their processes encoded in stable, faithful form, they being to ask "Why are we doing this? What happened here? What caused this change? What can we do differently?"

Without data, the answer to these questions are anecdotal and hard to verify. Seen this way:

> The value of data is not that it provides answers; but that it stops people from guessing.

Because of this, an often under-appreciated ability of data is, not its ability to tell stories, but its ability to eliminate false ones.

More than once, I have seen leadership groups fall unaware into a false assumption. Reality is complex, and when things are complex, simple narratives are attractive—they describe the symptoms, seem coherent, and not obviously wrong. But they are mismatched with reality. When that happens, even costly mistakes can be made and the leadership can even stay oblivious to them, because they were based on false assumptions in the first place.

I am brought in by a client, either consciously or fortuitously, for my data expertise. What I did was to pinpoint the source of confusion, and reconstruct a picture of the data that dismantled the false narrative that has taken hold, while offering a view of another root cause.

There is no glamour in eliminating a false narrative, but it is exactly the capability necessary in an organisation dealing with high-stakes situations: and this is what unglamorous data—data that is silently operative and faithfully representing the business—provides.

Unglamorous data is also where a lot of organisations go stray. They invest large amount of money into data capability but find that the investment does not translate into value. This is because they are asking the data to be *insightful*—and disappointed to find it is not—whereas the need is to ensure that data is *operable*. Data that may seem insignificant but meaningfully operative is more valuable to an organisation than significant data sitting in a data team.

Glamorous data—data that is insightful—is important, but unglamorous data—ordinary data that is meaningfully operative—is equally, if not more, so. 

Data is the eyes and ears of modern organisations. At scale, it is the only way [reality remains visible]{{< relref "blind-spots.md" >}}).
