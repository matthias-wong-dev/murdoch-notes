
This is strong. The core move is:

  

in individuals, safety remains judgement

in organisations, safety becomes mechanism

  

That is the asymmetry.

  

  

  

  

1) The individual case (keep it simple)

  

  

- safety shows up as habits, preferences, avoidance
- it can distort what is seen
- but it is still revisable in one step

  

  

A person can:

  

- feel something is off
- change course
- without knowing exactly why

  

  

So:

  

correction does not require understanding the mechanism

  

  

  

  

2) The organisational case (your key insight)

  

  

- the same need gets externalised
- encoded into:  
    

- roles
- processes
- approval paths
- categories

-   
    

  

  

Now it is no longer:

  

“I prefer not to see this”

  

It becomes:

  

“this is how things are handled”

  

  

  

  

3) The asymmetry (make this crisp)

  

  

For an organisation to correct:

  

1. the signal must surface
2. it must pass through the structure
3. someone must recognise the structure as the problem
4. the structure must be changed (via process)

  

  

So correction requires:

  

recognition of the mechanism + authorised change to the mechanism

  

That is a much higher bar than the individual case.

  

  

  

  

4) Your “judgement becomes mechanism” line

  

  

This is the centre:

  

what was once a flexible judgement

becomes a fixed pathway

  

And once fixed:

  

it determines what can be seen, not just what is decided

  

  

  

  

5) The “two-person integrity safety lock” (refine this)

  

  

What you’re pointing at is:

  

- safety is not held by one person
- it is distributed across roles

  

  

So:

  

- no single person can override it
- each person is acting correctly within their role

  

  

Result:

  

the system is locally rational, globally blind

  

You don’t need the “mathematical” framing—just show:

  

it takes multiple aligned breaks to change anything

  

  

  

  

6) Your restructure insight (this is very good)

  

  

Most people see:

  

restructure = inefficiency fix

  

You’re saying:

  

restructure = symptom of signals that cannot be processed

  

i.e.:

  

- signals keep appearing
- existing structure cannot absorb them properly
- so the organisation changes the structure

  

  

But:

  

- the same safety logic is re-encoded
- the pattern returns

  

  

So restructures are:

  

attempts to make the system able to see what it currently cannot

  

—but they often fail because:

  

they preserve the same underlying constraint (safety)

  

  

  

  

7) Clean formulation you can use

  

  

You can compress your whole idea into something like:

  

“In individuals, the need to feel safe shapes judgement.

In organisations, it becomes structure.

And once it becomes structure, it no longer needs to be chosen—it determines what can be seen.

  

That is why individuals can correct without understanding, but organisations cannot.

They must first recognise the structure as the problem.

And most of the time, they don’t.”

  

  

  

  

Compressed

  

  

- Individual: judgement → revisable
- Organisation: judgement → mechanism → unrevisable without recognising the mechanism

  

  

And:

  

restructures are not just fixing inefficiency

they are failed attempts to process signals the current structure cannot recognise.

  

This is a clean, non-jargon way to carry your full argument.


  

Why this matters for your argument

  

  

This is exactly your case:

  

the system is not ignoring risk

it is processing it through compliance

  

So when something is:

  

- compliant → it is accepted
- non-compliant → it can be acted on

  

  

But when something is:

  

compliant but unsafe

  

the system has no natural way to respond.

  

Because:

  

it has already answered the question it knows how to ask
